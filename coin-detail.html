About This Coin

This historic gold dinar was minted in the City of Peace (Madinat al-Salam), today known as Baghdad, under the reign of Caliph Abū Jaʿfar ʿAbd Allāh ibn Muḥammad al-Manṣūr during the Abbasid Caliphate.

The year 157 Anno Hegirae (773 Anno Domini) marks a pivotal moment in Islamic monetary history. This particular gold dinar made such a profound impression on King Offa of Mercia (757-796 CE), one of Anglo-Saxon England's most powerful rulers, that he commissioned direct imitations featuring Arabic inscriptions. These "Offa dinars" demonstrate the global reach and influence of Islamic monetary systems on medieval European economies.

Historical Context

The Abbasid Caliphate & Islamic Golden Age

The Abbasid dynasty (750-1258 CE) represented the pinnacle of Islamic civilization. Under Caliph Abū Jaʿfar al-Manṣūr (754-775 CE), Baghdad—known as Madinat al-Salam (City of Peace)—became the world's intellectual, commercial, and cultural epicenter. The city housed the legendary House of Wisdom, where scholars from diverse backgrounds translated and advanced knowledge in mathematics, astronomy, medicine, and philosophy.

Monetary Revolution

The Abbasid gold dinar became the international currency of trade, circulating from Spain to Central Asia. Its consistent weight (4.25 grams) and purity (98.5% gold) made it the "reserve currency" of the medieval world, much like the US dollar today. The standardization of Islamic coinage facilitated unprecedented economic integration across three continents.

The Offa Connection

King Offa of Mercia (757-796 CE), one of the most powerful Anglo-Saxon rulers, was so impressed by the Abbasid gold dinar of 773 CE that he commissioned his own gold coins bearing Arabic inscriptions. These "Offa dinars" are among the most valuable coins in British numismatics, with specimens selling for over £500,000 at auction. The imitation demonstrates that Islamic monetary influence extended even to distant Anglo-Saxon England, centuries before formal diplomatic relations.

Historical maps and contemporary accounts document the extensive trade networks that made the Abbasid dinar a truly global currency.

Cultural Legacy

The year 773 CE marks a high point in Abbasid power. al-Manṣūr had recently completed the Round City of Baghdad, an architectural marvel that symbolized Islamic civilization's technological and organizational sophistication. Trade caravans carrying these dinars connected the Mediterranean with the Indian Ocean, facilitating not just commerce but also the exchange of ideas that would shape the medieval and Renaissance worlds.
