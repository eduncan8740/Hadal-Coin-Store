Our Heritage

Preserving the numismatic legacy of great Islamic and African civilizations

The Abbasid Caliphate

The Abbasid dynasty (750-1258 CE) represented the pinnacle of the Islamic Golden Age. Under Caliph Abū Jaʿfar al-Manṣūr, Baghdad—known as Madinat al-Salam (City of Peace)—became the world's intellectual, commercial, and cultural epicenter. The gold dinar of 157 AH (773 CE) influenced European monetary systems for centuries, with King Offa of Mercia creating direct imitations that bore Arabic inscriptions.

The Kingdom of Aksum

The Aksumite Empire (100-940 CE), also known as the Kingdom of Aksum, was one of the four great powers of the ancient world alongside Rome, Persia, and China. Located in modern-day Ethiopia and Eritrea, this African civilization controlled vital Red Sea trade routes and minted some of the continent's finest ancient gold coinage, featuring both Christian and pre-Christian religious symbolism.

Cross-Cultural Influence

King Offa of Mercia (757-796 CE), one of Anglo-Saxon England's most powerful rulers, was so profoundly impressed by the Abbasid gold dinar of 157 AH / 773 AD that he commissioned direct imitations featuring Arabic inscriptions. These "Offa dinars" demonstrate the global reach and influence of Islamic monetary systems on medieval European economies and the interconnected nature of 8th-century trade networks.
